For my stack overflow questions, I decided to look for ones relating to unity which I am familiar with.
## Smart Question
"Unity Scripts edited in Visual studio don't provide autocomplete" From the surface level, this question looks to be relatively basic and the headline could almost be considered a "not smart" question, however after reading through the post and replies I believe this is a thoughtful question. The author first provides a short description of their problem - "When I want to edit C# Unity scripts, they open in Visual Studio. It is supposed to provide auto complete for all Unity related code, but it doesn't work." - and then shares a embedded gif of their exact problem. They also include at the bottom of their post all the versions of the problematic software to help people answering understand. The responses are also very well written with multiple answers that include step-by-step instructions with images and videos of how exactly to fix it.

[link here](https://stackoverflow.com/questions/42597501/unity-scripts-edited-in-visual-studio-dont-provide-autocomplete/42598927#42598927)
## "Not Smart" Question
"I'm trying to make a round system where once a round has been defeated another one begins but don't know how to detect when the enemies are destroyed" This is a good example of a "not smart" question. This is an example of a bad question because the author's title does not really relate comepletely to their code and they also do not provide a deeper explanation of what they want. As one of the answers points out as well, the code they shared doesn't really make sense and probably wouldn't even compile in its current state.

[link here](https://stackoverflow.com/questions/77038463/im-trying-to-make-a-round-system-where-once-a-round-has-been-defeated-another-o)
## My Thoughts
Smart questions are an intersting topic I did not think we would go over in this class. I found them to be interesting though, because I have definitely asked a few dumb questions in the past on forum websites/communites. After reading through the essay and many stack overflow responses, I've learned how asking a "smart question" can lead you to more helpful and detailed answers.
